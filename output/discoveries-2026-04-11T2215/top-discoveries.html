This challenges the common coaching advice to "be aggressive at the kitchen" — the data suggests speed-ups are often shot selection errors, not tactical advantages.

SUPPORTED — With Caveats
"Advanced players have distinct shot sequence signatures that could power a coaching product"
The data supports this hypothesis with moderate confidence across three independent signals. First, the 4.0+ fingerprint (speed_up→block→speed_up chain) appears exclusively in advanced player footage — a concrete, teachable 3-shot sequence. Second, the dink-to-attack conversion rate is measurably higher in 3.5+ players, demonstrating a behavioral distinction in kitchen strategy. Third, kitchen mastery rating tracks DUPR band in a linear, monotonic progression.

However, three structural limitations prevent a "strongly supported" verdict: (1) All clips are from a single venue — likely 2-3 recurring advanced players explain the 4.0 patterns. (2) Highlights are non-random — longer rallies are preferentially captured, skewing rally length findings. (3) DUPR estimates are AI-generated from the same model doing the analysis — circular reasoning risk.

Verdict: The signatures are real and demonstrable. Whether they're generalizable enough to power a commercial coaching product requires 200+ clips across 3+ venues and verified DUPR scores.
