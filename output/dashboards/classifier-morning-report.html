high rule effectiveness Extreme Rule Imbalance - JOOLA Dominates Classification
Evidence: JOOLA rule fires 3,295 times while the second-highest (Nike) fires only 319 times, representing 87% of all rule firings across 49 rules.
Action: Investigate JOOLA rule for overfitting or false positives; consider splitting into more granular sub-rules or reviewing its matching pattern for unintended scope.
high taxonomy gap Venue Equipment Dominates Fallback (80.6%)
Evidence: 274 of 340 fallback items (80.6%) are categorized as venue_equipment, suggesting the category is either too broad or lacks sufficient sub-categories.
Action: Decompose venue_equipment into sport-specific or function-specific subcategories (e.g., gym_equipment, field_equipment, seating, infrastructure).
high rule effectiveness Severely Underutilized Rules
Evidence: Three rules (Selkirk, CRBN, H2) fire only 1-3 times total across the entire dataset.
Action: Review these rules' matching criteria to determine if they are too restrictive, obsolete, or targeting edge cases; consider consolidating or removing if no longer needed.
high taxonomy gap Unrecognized Brand Names Suggest Missing Entity Rules
Evidence: Top fallback brand 'R&M_foz_wifi_goomba' (30 hits) appears nonsensical; research identified 10 real brands in the queue, indicating the classifier lacks patterns for emerging or variant brand name formats.
Action: Implement fuzzy matching or alternate name rules for known brands, and establish a weekly brand discovery workflow for the 50-item investigate queue.
high decision pattern Strong bias toward niche/characteristic-driven approvals
Evidence: 49 approvals with zero sponsor subtypes indicates Bill filters by product characteristics (paddle, apparel, shoes) rather than brand partnerships or commercial relationships.
Action: Classifier should weight product-level attributes (material, function, specificity) over sponsor tier or brand recognition; flag generic/broad sponsor categories for additional scrutiny.
high decision pattern Pending investigations suggest verification-seeking behavior
Evidence: 50 items pending investigation vs. 49 approved indicates Bill uses 'pending' as a holding state to gather consensus and harden ideas before committing.
Action: Surface multi-perspective data during investigation phase: alternative category tags, similar historical decisions, and secondary expert opinions to enable faster consensus-building.
medium decision pattern Extremely low rejection rate signals high approval threshold clarity
Evidence: Only 2 rejections across 101 classified items (1.98%) with 3 overrides suggests Bill has strong, consistent criteria but may use overrides to re-examine borderline cases.
Action: Extract decision rules from the 2 rejected items and 3 override cases; create explicit rubric for 'characteristics-based approval' to reduce pending queue and catch edge cases earlier.
medium taxonomy gap Ambiguous Single-Letter Brand Codes
Evidence: Fallback names 'W', 'ACE', 'WD' (single letters or abbreviations with 4–10 hits each) lack sufficient context to distinguish brands from typos or abbreviations.
Action: Create validation rules to flag single-letter or 2-letter brand tokens and require manual review or context-dependent disambiguation.
medium decision pattern Revenue potential decoupled from approval likelihood
Evidence: High-revenue items approved (18) but also 12 'none' revenue items approved; no correlation visible between revenue tier and approval, confirming characteristics-over-commercial preference.
Action: Remove revenue potential from primary classifier features; instead use product category specificity, uniqueness, and functional differentiation as primary signals.
medium rule effectiveness Wide Distribution Gap Across Mid-Tier Rules
Evidence: Rules 3-10 show declining fire counts from 116 down to 21, with no clear clustering pattern.
Action: Analyze whether rules 3-10 are addressing distinct categories or if some overlap exists; consolidate overlapping rules to reduce rule count and improve maintainability.
medium taxonomy gap Cross-Sport Misclassification Risk
Evidence: Research found 5 cross-sport items in the queue, indicating the classifier may not handle multi-sport or sport-agnostic equipment correctly.
Action: Add a 'multi_sport' or 'cross_sport' category, or create sport-independent parent categories for shared equipment (e.g., bags, protective gear).
low rule effectiveness No Dead Rules Detected
Evidence: All 49 rules have fired at least once (never_fired_count = 0), indicating no completely unused rules.
Action: Continue monitoring low-firing rules quarterly; establish a minimum firing threshold (e.g., 5+ fires) to identify candidates for deprecation.
