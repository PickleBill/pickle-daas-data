Evidence

Turn the corpus into a real-time player valuation engine. Amateur league organizers pay to auto-draft balanced teams using AI skill scores. Each drafted player generates new video uploads (trying to improve their rating), creating a flywheel: more videos → better models → more accurate valuations → more leagues → more videos.

Devil's Advocate

high risk

Market execution risk: league organizers adopt the system, teams drafted as 'balanced' via AI are actually mismatched. Word spreads that auto-drafts produce worse play experience. Churn and refund liability. The corpus is a convenience sample, not a population census.

Independent Validation

partial

Would change mind: Stronger evidence than #1 but still thin. Would shift confidence with: (a) pilot with 2-3 amateur leagues showing 30%+ adoption for draft tool, (b) willingness-to-pay study showing organizers pay $100-500/season, (c) comparison to existing fantasy sports pricing models. Assumes amateur league organizers have budget and care about balance; needs validation.

Blind spot: Ignores that pickleball leagues may already solve balance manually or via cheap/free alternatives (handicap systems, snake drafts). Also: skill scoring accuracy is critical but untested—wrong valuations destroy trust. Assumes skill metadata is granular enough to predict team chemistry, which video clip data alone may not capture.
